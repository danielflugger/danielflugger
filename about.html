About

Builder by nature.
Engineer by practice.

I'm Daniel Flügger — an applied AI engineer with extensive experience building across music, design, and machine learning. I'm based in New York, currently in the Google Startups Accelerator, and looking for the right full-time engineering role where I can contribute inside a strong team while continuing to build independently.

The Work

What I actually do.

My current technical focus is GCP-native AI systems: geospatial intelligence pipelines using PostGIS and BigQuery, retrieval-augmented generation architectures with Vertex AI and the Gemini API, and multi-agent orchestration systems built on FastAPI and Cloud Run.

I don't just build prototypes — I build systems designed to operate in production, handle edge cases, and be maintained by someone other than me. Verification, observability, and data sovereignty are built in from day one, not bolted on after.

The VERA framework and the Proof Economy essay came out of this practice — my attempt to codify what I've learned about building trustworthy AI systems and what that means for knowledge workers and enterprises navigating this transition.

What I Bring

The operator's advantage.

Most engineers understand the technology. Fewer understand the domain problems the technology is supposed to solve. I bring both.

  • Extensive cross-domain building experience — I've operated in music, design, real estate, and AI. I understand enterprise buyers from the inside.
  • GCP-native depth — not just API calls. Proper architecture with IAM, Cloud Run, BigQuery, and Vertex AI working together.
  • Spatial intelligence specialization — property, infrastructure, logistics, and insurance domains where location is the core data layer.
  • Founder's judgment — I've shipped products and learned from what didn't work. I bring that cost-awareness into every engineering decision.
  • Verification-first methodology — I don't trust outputs I can't trace. VERA is the formal expression of how I work.

The Longer Arc

Twenty years of pattern recognition.

I started in music production and design — industries where you learn very quickly that the tools don't matter as much as the taste, judgment, and problem framing you bring to them. That carried forward into software.

The common thread across everything I've built is a preoccupation with the gap between what systems promise and what they actually deliver — and a determination to close that gap through verification, observability, and honest engineering.

The Proof Economy essay is my thesis on where this leads economically: in a world of abundant AI capability, the scarce resource is verified, provable quality. VERA is the engineering methodology for building it.

I'm sharing both publicly not to position myself as a thought leader, but because I think the ideas are genuinely useful and I'd rather they be in the world than locked on my hard drive.
